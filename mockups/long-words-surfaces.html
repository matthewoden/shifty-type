Alternatives, before we commit to the fold

The detail card is the stress case — full-size tiles, the place you study a word before challenging it. Here it is four ways. C is swipeable and D is tappable; try them.

A · The fold as proposed

+ Every letter visible at full size, no gesture needed — you can rule on a spelling at a glance. – The word becomes a paragraph; three lines for the monster.

B · Shrink to fit

+ One word, one line — the shape and length read instantly. – 11px tiles: you can count the letters but barely read them, and the chiclets stop being chiclets.

C · Ride the rail swipe it

+ Full-size tiles, one line, and the gesture is the board's own camera move. – Most letters hidden at rest — to check a spelling you must swipe, and a side-scroll inside a bottom sheet fights the sheet's own gestures.

D · The squeeze tap it

+ Compact at rest — head and tail (where the grips live) stay readable, the middle concertinas. Tap and it fans open into the fold. – Two-state UI on a receipt, and the letters that matter in a challenge are exactly the hidden ones.

The lens that decides it: this is a game about challenging exact spellings. Any treatment that hides letters at rest (C, D — and ellipsis truncation, not shown) undermines the one decision these cards exist to support. That leaves A and B — and B gives up readability for a single-line purity nobody is scoring. Plain text instead of tiles was also considered and dropped: the chiclets are the word's identity everywhere else.

Where C could still earn a place: the final scoreboard list, where nothing is at stake and one-row-per-word keeps the recap scannable. Shown small below — swipeable too:

C´ · Scoreboard rows on the rail

Each long row scrolls on its own under an edge fade; short rows don't move. Versus the fold: tidier column, but the recap becomes something you operate instead of read.
